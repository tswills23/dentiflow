"We need more new patients."

No. You need to answer the phone when the ones you're paying for actually call.

Peerlogic just published a study from February 2026. They tracked every inbound call across 26 dental practices.

4,280 calls in one month.

38% went unanswered.

Not after hours. Not on weekends. During business hours, with staff in the building....

Of the calls that did get answered, new patient conversion was 25%. One in four. The other three heard something that made them hang up or not book.

So out of every 100 inbound calls, 62 get answered. Of those 62, roughly 15 become patients.

85 out of 100 callers did not book.

Harvard studied speed-to-lead across industries. Responding within 60 seconds of first contact increased close rates by 391%.

Not 39%.

THREE HUNDRED AND NINETY-ONE PERCENT.

Most dental practices don't respond within 60 minutes, let alone 60 seconds.

If you're spending $5,000 to $10,000 a month on marketing, and 38% of those calls go to voicemail, you're lighting $1,900 to $3,800 on fire every month.

$22,800 to $45,600 per year. Burned before your front desk even gets involved.

The practices that figured this out didn't hire more staff. They didn't increase their ad budget. They fixed the gap between the phone ringing and a human responding.

Somewhere right now, a practice owner is refreshing their Google Ads dashboard while the phone rings to voicemail in the other room.

How many calls went to voicemail at your practice last week?
